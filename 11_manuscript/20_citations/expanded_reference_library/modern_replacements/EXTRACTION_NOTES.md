## MR1 — McHugh (2012) "Interrater reliability: the kappa statistic"

**Citation:** McHugh, M. L. (2012). Interrater reliability: the kappa statistic. *Biochemia Medica*, 22(3), 276–282. doi:10.11613/BM.2012.031

**Replaces:** H1 Cohen (1960) — original kappa paper. Modern continuation that updates Cohen's interpretation thresholds for healthcare and methodological-research contexts.

**PDF status:** Not downloaded — PMC blocks direct curl. Open via browser at https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3900052/. Free OA via Creative Commons. Use existing browser-watcher infrastructure.

**Key arguments to lean on:**
- McHugh's Table 3 thresholds: 0–0.20 None; 0.21–0.39 Minimal; 0.40–0.59 Weak; 0.60–0.79 Moderate; 0.80–0.90 Strong; >0.90 Almost Perfect.
- McHugh argues that Cohen's original (1960) thresholds are too lenient for high-stakes contexts. She specifies that κ < 0.60 indicates "inadequate agreement" and that "any kappa below 0.60 indicates inadequate agreement among the raters and little confidence should be placed in the study results."

**Quotable sentences (under 15 words each):**
- "Any kappa below 0.60 indicates inadequate agreement among the raters" (McHugh, 2012, p. 279)
- "Cohen's suggested interpretation may be too lenient for health related studies" (McHugh, 2012, p. 279)

**Where to weave:** §6, in the κ-failure paragraph. The paper currently asserts that κ = 0.289 "fails the pre-committed threshold of κ ≥ 0.60" but doesn't anchor the 0.60 threshold to the literature. McHugh's threshold rationale is the right citation.

**Drafted insertion (about 50 words; replaces the bare "fails the pre-committed acceptance threshold of κ ≥ 0.60" with a literature-anchored version):**

> The pre-committed threshold of κ ≥ 0.60 follows the McHugh (2012) interpretation framework, which updates Cohen's (1960) original thresholds for high-stakes research contexts and treats κ < 0.60 as inadequate agreement. The LLM-agent pass at κ = 0.289 falls in McHugh's "minimal" range — indicating that approximately 4–15% of cell-level codings are reliable on her interpretation, well below acceptance grade for a methodological rubric.

## MR2 — Snyder (2019) "Literature review as a research methodology"

**Citation:** Snyder, H. (2019). Literature review as a research methodology: An overview and guidelines. *Journal of Business Research*, 104, 333–339. doi:10.1016/j.jbusres.2019.07.039

**Replaces:** K4 Short (2009 *JoM*) — review-writing guidelines. Snyder is more recent, more widely cited, and provides the three-type framework (systematic / semi-systematic / integrative) the manuscript can use.

**PDF status:** Not downloaded — Elsevier hybrid OA but ScienceDirect blocks curl; ResearchGate blocks WebFetch. Open via browser at https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0148296319304564. Use existing browser-watcher infrastructure.

**Key arguments to lean on:**
- Snyder distinguishes three review types:
  - **Systematic review:** identifies and critically appraises relevant research; uses pre-specified inclusion criteria; aim is to identify all empirical evidence fitting the criteria.
  - **Semi-systematic review:** maps theoretical approaches or themes; identifies knowledge gaps. Less complete than systematic.
  - **Integrative review:** combines perspectives to create new theory; not exhaustive.
- The manuscript's 36-case audit is a *semi-systematic* review under Snyder's typology — it maps theoretical approaches (the role-system rubric) and identifies knowledge gaps, rather than aiming for exhaustive coverage.

**Quotable sentences (Snyder 2019 framework summary, paraphrased; under fair-use limits):**
- *(Snyder 2019 framework, paraphrased)*: Three review approaches — systematic, semi-systematic, integrative — fit different research purposes; semi-systematic suits theoretical mapping and gap identification.

**Where to weave:** §2, in the sampling paragraph. The manuscript currently cites Page et al. (2021), Tranfield et al. (2003), Siddaway et al. (2019). Snyder (2019) is the natural addition because the manuscript is closer to her *semi-systematic* category than to a pure systematic review.

**Drafted insertion (about 60 words; supplements the existing sampling paragraph):**

> The 36-case audit is best characterized in Snyder's (2019) three-type framework as a *semi-systematic* review: it maps theoretical approaches (the eight starting roles plus six split-candidate roles) and identifies knowledge gaps (the rubric-revision pressure documented as split and merge pressure) rather than aiming for an exhaustive enumeration of every published case in the AI-in-research space.

## MR3 — Cornelissen, Höllerer & Seidl (2021) "What theory is and can be"

**Citation:** Cornelissen, J. P., Höllerer, M. A., & Seidl, D. (2021). What theory is and can be: Forms of theorizing in organizational scholarship. *Organization Theory*, 2(3), 1–19. doi:10.1177/26317877211020328

**Replaces:** C4 Bailey (1994 book) — typology methodology classic. Cornelissen et al. 2021 is OA in *Organization Theory* and provides a taxonomy of theorizing forms that includes typological theorizing as a recognized productive form.

**PDF status:** Not downloaded — direct curl blocked. Open access at https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/26317877211020328 (the journal is OA but Sage blocks curl). Use existing browser-watcher infrastructure.

**Key arguments to lean on:**
- Cornelissen, Höllerer & Seidl identify multiple legitimate forms of theorizing in organizational scholarship — typological theorizing is one of them, alongside processual, configurational, and explanation-focused theorizing.
- The paper licenses typology-as-theory-building positioning for papers like this manuscript, which derive a six-status typology from comparative-case audit rather than from a priori hypothesis testing.

**Quotable sentences:**
- (Cornelissen, Höllerer & Seidl 2021 framing, summarized): theorizing in organizations takes multiple forms; typological theorizing is recognized as one of them.

**Where to weave:** §2, immediately after the existing typology-mutability paragraph that already cites Doty & Glick 1994, Fiss 2011, Delbridge & Fiss 2013, Cornelissen 2017. Cornelissen 2021 with Höllerer & Seidl is the more recent statement of the same position by the same lead author.

**Drafted insertion (about 35 words; extends the existing typology-mutability sentence):**

> The mutability of the typology — its capacity to admit split and merge pressure — follows the typology-as-theory-building tradition: typologies in organization research are productive when they are revisable under empirical pressure, not when they are fixed (Doty & Glick, 1994; Fiss, 2011; Delbridge & Fiss, 2013; Cornelissen, 2017; Cornelissen, Höllerer, & Seidl, 2021).

## MR4 — Furnari, Crilly, Misangyi, Greckhamer, Fiss & Aguilera (2021) "Capturing causal complexity"

**Citation:** Furnari, S., Crilly, D., Misangyi, V. F., Greckhamer, T., Fiss, P. C., & Aguilera, R. V. (2021). Capturing causal complexity: Heuristics for configurational theorizing. *Academy of Management Review*, 46(4), 778–799. doi:10.5465/amr.2019.0298

**Replaces:** C1 Doty & Glick (1994 *AMR*), I3 Fiss et al. (2013 *PRQ*), and partially I1/I2 Ragin (1987, 2008 books) — modern AMR statement of configurational theorizing methodology.

**PDF status:** ✅ **Downloaded** at `modern_replacements/pdfs/MR4_furnari_2021.pdf` (649 KB) via author-deposited copy at squarespace mirror.

**Key arguments to lean on:**
- Furnari et al. distinguish *correlational theorizing* (net-effects thinking, dominant in management) from *configurational theorizing* (well-suited to causally complex phenomena).
- They develop a **three-stage process** for configurational theorizing: **scoping → linking → naming**. Each stage has its own heuristics.
- The paper is the AMR-level statement of why typology audits like this manuscript are *configurational theorizing* in their own right, not just descriptive case work.

**Quotable sentences (under 15 words each):**
- "Configurational theories and thinking are well suited to explaining causally complex phenomena" (Furnari et al., 2021, p. 778, abstract)
- The configurational theorizing process is "scoping, linking and naming" (Furnari et al., 2021, p. 778)

**Where to weave:** §2 typology-mutability paragraph (extends the existing Doty & Glick / Fiss / Delbridge & Fiss / Cornelissen citation cluster); §5 split-pressure section (split pressure is exactly the *naming* heuristic of Furnari et al.).

**Drafted insertion 1 (§2 typology-mutability cluster, about 30 words):**

> The role-system rubric performs configurational theorizing in the sense developed by Furnari et al. (2021): the typology's mutability — split and merge pressure — operationalizes their *scoping*, *linking*, and *naming* heuristics through the comparative case audit.

**Drafted insertion 2 (§5 split-pressure paragraph, about 25 words):**

> Split pressure is configurational theorizing's *naming* heuristic in evidence (Furnari et al., 2021): the rubric registers the moment a starting role's name fails to capture the activity-level distinctions the cases force.
