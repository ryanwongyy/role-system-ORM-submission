**Date:** 2026-04-23

## 1. Scholarly contribution

**Headline:** The manuscript's contribution is narrower, more methodological, and more hedged than templates, which position their work as standard-setting or literature-bridging.

| Paper | Contribution claim | Gap positioning | Confidence register |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Manuscript v12** | Rubric for role-status coding + worked application to 36 cases | "Vocabulary used to adjudicate AI claims remains thin; debate oscillates between blanket displacement and continuity narratives" | Heavily hedged: "ceiling deliberately set at comparative typology audit"; "not immune to rival explanations"; κ reported as "limitation" |
| Aguinis 2023 | Best-practice rubric for methodological review quality | "Methodology reviews are increasingly common but lack a unified quality standard" | Confident: "fills an important gap"; presented as standard-setting |
| Solarino 2019 | 12 transparency criteria + BARS for qualitative research | "Transparency literature has focused on quantitative work" | Moderate: acknowledges ontological limits without hedging the instrument |
| Hickman 2022 | Decision framework for text preprocessing bridging computational linguistics and organizational practice | "Two literatures don't talk to each other" | Confident: "creates a bridge"; presented as solving a known problem |
| Aguinis-Villamor 2021 | 10 best-practice recommendations across 3 MTurk study stages | "MTurk use grew 2,117% but skepticism persists; no actionable guidance exists" | Confident: "catalyst for more robust research" |
| Tay 2022 | Conceptual framework for machine-learning measurement bias | "ML bias is documented empirically but lacks unified measurement perspective" | Confident but reflexive: "framework clarifies but does not reduce complexity" |

**The contrast.** Templates *assert what they accomplish*. The manuscript *apologizes for what it does not do* — "does not license claims that human research roles have broadly disappeared"; "claim ceiling is therefore deliberately set"; "we do not generalize beyond the sample." Every template gives readers a reason to adopt its deliverable before telling them what the deliverable cannot do; the manuscript presents the rubric and then, in the same sentence, often qualifies how to read it.

## 2. Referencing practices

**Headline:** The manuscript is radically under-referenced — 20 references vs. template norms of 80–150 — and 75% of those 20 references are its own cases, not scholarly literature.

| Metric | Manuscript v12 | Aguinis 2023 | Solarino 2019 | Hickman 2022 | Aguinis-Villamor 2021 | Tay 2022 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Total references | 20 | 100+ | 127 | 120+ | 144 | 80+ |
| In-text citations per 1,000 words | ~1.8 | ~8–10 | ~9–11 | ~10–12 | ~10 | ~7–8 |
| Reference-type balance | 75% cases (own dataset); 25% theory anchors | ~80% methodological/empirical; ~20% theoretical | ~60% empirical/methodological; ~40% conceptual | ~70% computational linguistics + text mining; ~30% organizational behavior | ~90% empirical/methodological; ~10% conceptual | ~60% empirical/ML; ~40% measurement theory |
| Self-citation | Minimal | Moderate (3–4) | Moderate (2–3) | Low–moderate | High (Aguinis frame recurring) | Low |
| Citation register | Integral theoretical positioning ("Abbott 1988 works at the occupational profession; we rescale") | Gap-then-solution ("Prior work shows X; we provide best practices") | Contrast-then-expand ("In contrast to quantitative… our contribution expands") | Bridging ("Two literatures; we integrate") | Procedural ("While recommendations may apply elsewhere, we focus specifically on MTurk") | Integrative ("We draw connections to existing psychological literature") |

**The quantitative gap.** At ~1.8 in-text citations per 1,000 words, the manuscript cites literature at roughly one-fifth to one-sixth the density of every template. At 20 total references, it would be the lowest-referenced paper in the ORM corpus by a large margin.

**The structural gap.** The manuscript's reference list reads as **a case inventory with theoretical anchors sprinkled in** rather than as a literature-review scaffold. Templates use citations to (a) establish what prior work has found, (b) identify the gap, (c) credit methodological precedents they're building on, and (d) situate each analytic move in an existing tradition. The manuscript does (d) for four anchors (Abbott, Freidson, Grimmer, Amershi + Shneiderman) and skips (a)–(c) almost entirely. Specifically missing: any ORM reference beyond the introductory mention of Aguinis 2023 and Hickman 2022; any SMJ, AMJ, JAP, or AMR precedent for typology-audit work; any references to the AI-in-social-science literature beyond the 15 coded cases.

**Consequence for reviewers.** A reviewer opening the reference list sees case codes (C001–C034) and five theorists. They do not see the paper's position in ORM's tradition of rubric-building, measurement-discipline methodology, or jurisdictional analysis. The reference list signals "database documentation," not "contribution to a scholarly literature."

## 3. Argumentation

**Headline:** Templates use explicit problem → gap → solution → evidence → implications arcs with every section announcing itself; the manuscript defers its argumentative payoff to §3 and its rival-explanations treatment to §6, which reverses the conventional flow.

| Feature | Manuscript v12 | Template norm |
|---|---|---|
| Argument structure | Cases → rubric → status counts → durable roles (§3) → shrinkage (§4) → splits/merges (§5) → caveats (§6) → conclusion | Problem → gap → solution → application → findings → implications |
| Section motivation | Mostly absent — §3 title is descriptive, no sentence explains *why* this section exists or what readers should expect | Each section announces itself: "We developed…"; "To test this, we…"; "Within this framework we define…"; "Our goal is to provide…" |
| Counterargument treatment | Deferred to §6 after all positive findings; rival explanations are named there for the first time | Integrated: each recommendation or claim addresses its own validity threat in the same section |
| Conclusion strength | Moderate: enumerates what the paper "can say" (H1 broadly supported, H2 conditionally, H3 as repository pattern) and "should not say" | Strong + forward-looking: "recommendations will improve transparency"; "we hope this article will serve as a catalyst"; "new procedures need development" |
| Positioning move | Mixed — "Unlike Aguinis et al. 2023…" (contrast) in some places; "Integrating across three levels, the rubric rescales their control-point intuitions" (cumulative) in others | Template-specific: Aguinis 2023 cumulative, Solarino 2019 contrast-heavy with cumulative close, Hickman 2022 bridging, Aguinis-Villamor 2021 procedural |

**Where the manuscript argues best.** §3's durable-roles pattern is the paper's strongest empirical move — 15 robust + 5 conditional + 7 transformed on *protocol and provenance architect* alone, with zero shrinking. But §3 does not announce itself as the paper's central finding; it opens with "§3.0 Exemplars" (a navigational subsection) and then presents counts. The reader has to infer that this is the crux.

**Where the manuscript argues weakest.** §6 takes back what §§3–5 gave. The rival-explanations list — publication bias, repository coding bias, benchmark artificiality, confidence stratification, inter-coder κ — runs ahead of any re-assertion of the paper's contribution. A reader who reads §6 in isolation would think the paper is fragile; only a reader who has already absorbed §§3–5 understands §6 as principled caution rather than concession.

**The missing conventional move.** Templates conventionally close with forward-looking implications. The manuscript's §7 ends by asserting the framework's use for three audiences (methods researchers, reviewers/editors, institutional actors) and then with the chiasmus "not from human research to machine research, but from one research role system to another." This is rhetorically strong but not forward-looking in the template sense — it doesn't name what research *next*.

## 4. Coherence / flow

**Headline:** Templates use argument-titled headings, numbered contributions, and explicit forward-pointing transitions ("Having established X, we now turn to Y"); the manuscript uses descriptive headings and mixes main-level structure with unnumbered procedural sections.

| Feature | Manuscript v12 | Template norm |
|---|---|---|
| Heading style | Mix: descriptive ("Data and Design," "Exemplars") + argument-titled ("Durable Roles: Procedure, Boundary Setting, and Calibration") | Consistent within each paper; usually argument-titled (Hickman, Tay) or standardly procedural (Solarino's INTRODUCTION / METHOD / RESULTS / DISCUSSION) |
| Main-level numbering | Inconsistent: §§1–7 numbered, plus two unnumbered main-level headings ("Data and Code Availability," "Transparency and Disclosures") embedded between §5 and §6 | Consistent numbering throughout; procedural material either in numbered subsections or in clearly separated appendices |
| Sub-heading depth | Mixed: §3 has §3.0 and §3.1 sub-subsections; other sections don't | Usually flat — every main section at the same depth, sub-sections only where genuinely warranted |
| Transitions between sections | Redundant rather than bridging: §2 ends "Table 1 specifies the rubric used to code it"; §3 begins "Before turning to the durable-role pattern…" — no forward-pointing logic | Explicit bridging: "Having described X, we now turn to Y"; "To test this, we…"; templates actively hand the reader from one section to the next |
| Internal recapitulation | Limited: numbered hypotheses (H1, H2, H3) stated in §1 but no running tally section-by-section ("so far we've found H1 supported on X, Y, Z") | Heavy: templates periodically recall where the argument is ("Our results uncovered that insufficient transparency is as pervasive in qualitative research as it has been documented in quantitative research (Aguinis et al., 2018)…") |
| Narrative arc | Uneven — climax at §3, then §4–5 feel additive, §6 defensive | Problem → gap → solution → application → validation → implications, with each section reinforcing the arc |
| Footnote register | Heavy methodological hedging inline and in extended caveats ("approximately 28 of the 612 cells are tagged…"; "this tighter tie-breaker was introduced in response to…") | Lighter, clarifying rather than defensive |

**Diagnostic sentence.** The manuscript is *internally coherent* (the case-by-case logic tracks), but it is *rhetorically diffuse* — readers don't encounter "why this matters" strongly until §3, and once they do, §6 undercuts the stakes before §7 tries to re-assert them.

## Top 3 dimension-level gaps, ranked by reviewer impact

### 1. Reference gap (highest reviewer impact)

Twenty references — 15 of them case codes, 5 theoretical anchors — against template norms of 80–150. A reviewer opening the reference list will read the paper as **a database documentation exercise**, not as **a contribution to the ORM tradition of rubric-building and best-practice frameworks**. The manuscript names Aguinis 2023 and Hickman 2022 in §1 but does not carry them or any other ORM precedent into the reference list. Missing: methodological-audit precedents in ORM / SMJ / AMJ / AMR / JAP; AI-in-social-science literature beyond the 15 coded cases; typology-audit methodology citations; jurisdictional-sociology citations beyond Abbott.

### 2. Argumentative sequencing (rhetorical clarity)

Templates put the gap and the solution in §1 so readers encounter the crux before the evidence. The manuscript reverses this: readers meet 36 cases and six status labels before understanding why role status matters as a frame. The durable-roles finding in §3 is the paper's strongest move but is not announced as such; §6's rival-explanations treatment arrives after the positive findings rather than integrated with them; §7 does not name forward-looking implications in the template sense.

### 3. Headings and signposting (reading experience)

The §3.0 and §3.1 sub-subsections in a seven-section paper, two unnumbered main-level headings between §5 and §6, and descriptive rather than argument-titled headings all signal a paper whose organization is still finding its shape. A reader of Hickman 2022 or Tay 2022 can predict from each section title what the section will argue; a reader of the manuscript cannot predict from "3.0 Exemplars" what the subsection's argumentative role is.

## Writer-voice diagnosis

The manuscript's voice is **methodologically meticulous, conceptually cautious, and rhetorically defensive**. It reads as a researcher who has spent substantial effort building a database (612 cells, inter-coder checks, sensitivity analyses, pre-analysis commitment) and is deeply aware of its own limitations, answering reviewer objections before the reviewer has raised them.

The template voices are categorically different:
- **Aguinis 2023** — confident proceduralist; "We conducted a five-step process…"
- **Solarino 2019** — transparent empiricist; null finding (no articles fully transparent) becomes evidence of the problem
- **Hickman 2022** — bridging theorist; integration of two literatures
- **Aguinis-Villamor 2021** — actionable pragmatist; "catalyst for more robust research"
- **Tay 2022** — clarifying systems-thinker; framework as integrative lens

ORM does value methodological rigor and transparency — the manuscript's voice is not *wrong* for the venue. But the voice **undermines itself** by announcing limitations before announcing accomplishments. A reviewer reading §6 first would think the paper is weak; a reviewer reading §1 would think the contribution is tentative; only a reader who works through §3 carefully would see the durable-roles pattern as the substantive finding.

**The summary.** The manuscript's data, rubric, and findings are sound. The *presentation* reads like a careful auditor documenting a dataset, not like a researcher claiming a methodological contribution to ORM's tradition. On all four writing dimensions — scholarly contribution, referencing practices, argumentation, coherence/flow — the distance from template norms is consequential and visible on first read.
