# Template Candidates for the Manuscript

**Date:** 2026-04-21
**Target manuscript:** [10_manuscript.md](../10_drafts/10_manuscript.md) — *From Repository to Role System: A Comparative Typology Audit of 36 Cases*
**Intended venue:** Organizational Research Methods (ORM)

## How these were selected

Filter criteria, in priority order:

1. **Venue match** — ORM preferred; allied venues (AMR, SMJ, JOM, *Academy of Management Annals*, *Sociological Methods & Research*, *Socius*, *Big Data & Society*) acceptable.
2. **Genre match** — comparative methodological framework or multi-case typology audit that categorises articles/cases against a coding scheme and derives recommendations. Not single-case studies, not pure benchmarks, not pure conceptual essays.
3. **Structural match** — abstract + intro + explicit design section + findings organised by category/status + rival-explanations/limits + bounded-claim conclusion.
4. **Quantitative apparatus** — contingency tables, association statistics, effect sizes. Bonus.
5. **Topic adjacency** — AI/ML in research workflows, computational social science, text mining, measurement discipline. Bonus.

Shortlist is ranked into two tiers. Tier 1 is what a careful ORM reviewer would most plausibly pull up as precedent next to the manuscript draft. Tier 2 is structurally further away but still useful for specific moves.

## Tier 1 — closest templates

### 1. Aguinis, Ramani & Alabduljader (2023), *ORM*

**Citation:** Aguinis, H., Ramani, R. S., & Alabduljader, N. (2023). Best-practice recommendations for producers, evaluators, and users of methodological literature reviews. *Organizational Research Methods*, 26(1), 46–76. DOI: [10.1177/1094428120943281](https://doi.org/10.1177/1094428120943281). **Local PDF:** [`template_pdfs/aguinis_ramani_alabduljader_2023_ORM_methodological_reviews_v1.pdf`](template_pdfs/aguinis_ramani_alabduljader_2023_ORM_methodological_reviews_v1.pdf) (also `_v2.pdf`).

**Why it's the single best template.** Same journal, same genre, same move: they categorise and content-analyse **168 methodological literature reviews** across **42 journals** against a **7-category typology** (critical, descriptive, meta-analytic, narrative, qualitative systematic, scoping, umbrella), derive **10 latent factors with 40 observable indicators**, and deliver an actionable **checklist** serving three stakeholder groups. Structurally this is exactly the move the P1 manuscript is trying to make with 36 cases × 17 roles × 6 statuses. Their three-part contribution statement maps directly onto the P1 paper's typology-audit + role-coding + anchor-cases structure.

**Features to borrow:**
- **The three-part contribution statement** in the abstract ("First… Second… Third…") and its echo at the end of the intro. Replace the P1 abstract's current thesis-first framing with a structured enumeration of categorisation / evidence / recommendations.
- **"Purpose and Approach" as a standalone opening section** (rather than a "Data and Design" section buried in §2). Pulls the reviewer into the categorical scheme early.
- **Exemplar-driven illustration of the typology** — for each status category, they pick 1–2 published reviews as exemplars. The P1 manuscript does this implicitly via Tornberg / Keith / Baccolini; making it systematic (e.g., "Exemplar of *robust* Protocol and provenance architect = Case 2 Keith 2026; exemplar of *shrinking* Routine coder = Case 1 Tornberg 2025") would tighten the argument.
- **Tripartite stakeholder framing** ("producers, evaluators, users") — the P1 paper gestures at researchers / reviewers / institutional actors but doesn't name them as a structured audience triad. Explicit naming is free rigour.
- **Output artifact paired with the framework** — they ship a checklist. The P1 equivalent is the released repository + codebook; that linkage should be foregrounded, not left to the data-availability statement.

**Risks / what *not* to borrow.** Their argument rests on *published* peer-reviewed reviews as the unit of analysis; their sampling frame is journal-visible. The P1 repository includes preprints, benchmark pages, and live-project sites — making the sampling frame more heterogenous and opening a rival-explanations angle they don't face.

### 2. Aguinis & Solarino (2019), *SMJ*

**Citation:** Aguinis, H., & Solarino, A. M. (2019). Transparency and replicability in qualitative research: The case of interviews with elite informants. *Strategic Management Journal*, 40(8), 1291–1315. DOI: [10.1002/smj.3015](https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.3015). **Local PDF:** [`template_pdfs/aguinis_solarino_2019_SMJ_transparency_replicability_elite_interviews.pdf`](template_pdfs/aguinis_solarino_2019_SMJ_transparency_replicability_elite_interviews.pdf).

**Why.** A methodological audit of **52 articles** from a single journal against a **12-criterion transparency framework** with **behaviorally-anchored rating scales (BARS)**. Concrete, evidence-driven audit of a comparable N (52 vs. your 36). Same methods-review genre but leaner and more surgical than the Aguinis et al. 2023 piece.

**Features to borrow:**
- **BARS for status levels.** The P1 role-coding rubric (`robust / conditional / transformed / shrinking / split_pressure / merge_pressure`) is defined prosaically in the codebook. Publishing a BARS-style table in the paper (concrete behavioural indicators for each status) would make the coding replicable for a reviewer without opening the repository.
- **Paired transparency framework + assessment protocol.** They ship both the criteria and the procedure to apply them. The P1 paper has the coding scheme but not an explicit inter-coder protocol; adding even a skeletal "how another coder would apply this" paragraph addresses the "single-coder" objection.
- **52-article sample size** anchors a comparator: if you want to defend 36 as adequate, cite this as precedent (SMJ accepted a comparable-N audit).

**Risks / what *not* to borrow.** SMJ's style is punchier and less hedged than ORM's; mimicking its conclusion voice could read as over-claiming for ORM reviewers.

### 3. Hickman, Thapa, Tay, Cao & Srinivasan (2022), *ORM*

**Citation:** Hickman, L., Thapa, S., Tay, L., Cao, M., & Srinivasan, P. (2022). Text preprocessing for text mining in organizational research: Review and recommendations. *Organizational Research Methods*, 25(1), 114–146. DOI: [10.1177/1094428120971683](https://doi.org/10.1177/1094428120971683). **Local PDF:** [`template_pdfs/hickman_thapa_tay_cao_srinivasan_2022_ORM_text_preprocessing.pdf`](template_pdfs/hickman_thapa_tay_cao_srinivasan_2022_ORM_text_preprocessing.pdf).

**Why.** Same journal, text-mining / ML topic domain, review-and-recommendations genre. Organised around **two complementary reviews** (computational-linguistics + organisational text-mining) and derives **empirically grounded decision-making recommendations** that account for task type, research question, and dataset characteristics. Closest template on *topic* adjacency.

**Features to borrow:**
- **"Two complementary reviews"** as an organising move — the P1 paper could frame its analysis as (a) role status across cases, (b) status distribution across stages and capabilities (Tables 2a/2b), showing that the two lenses converge on the same answer.
- **Decision-factor framing.** They state recommendations *conditional on* moderators (text type, research question, dataset characteristics). The P1 paper's H1/H2/H3 already do this implicitly; making the conditional structure explicit in the conclusion — "For researchers in [stage], with [task properties], expect [role status pattern]" — would lift the utility of the typology substantially.
- **Calibrated hedging in ORM voice.** Good source for the exact register ORM reviewers expect when acknowledging scope limits.

**Risks.** Their review is about *tool choice*; the P1 paper is about *work-system change*. Don't let the template pull the prose toward methods-advice when the P1 thesis is diagnostic.

## Tier 2 — partial / adjacent

### 4. Aguinis, Villamor & Ramani (2021), *Journal of Management*

**Citation:** Aguinis, H., Villamor, I., & Ramani, R. S. (2021). MTurk research: Review and recommendations. *Journal of Management*, 47(4), 823–837. DOI: [10.1177/0149206320969787](https://doi.org/10.1177/0149206320969787). **Local PDF:** [`template_pdfs/aguinis_villamor_ramani_2021_JoM_mturk_review.pdf`](template_pdfs/aguinis_villamor_ramani_2021_JoM_mturk_review.pdf).

**Why.** Reviews **510 empirical papers from 15 journals**, organises recommendations by **research stage** (planning / implementation / reporting) — structurally identical to organising P1's typology around research stage (H3). JOM audience overlaps significantly with ORM's.

**Features to borrow:**
- **Stage-organised recommendations table** — maps neatly onto the P1 conclusion if you want to give reader-level take-aways grouped by research stage.
- **Short, checklist-style recommendations** — concrete, numbered, and directly usable.

**Risks.** JOM is a less methods-specialist venue; the review is less theoretically framed than an ORM paper would be.

### 5. Ziems, Held, Shaikh, Chen, Zhang & Yang (2024), *Computational Linguistics*

**Citation:** Ziems, C., Held, W., Shaikh, O., Chen, J., Zhang, Z., & Yang, D. (2024). Can large language models transform computational social science? *Computational Linguistics*, 50(1), 237–291. DOI: [10.1162/coli_a_00502](https://doi.org/10.1162/coli_a_00502). arXiv: [2305.03514](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.03514).

**Why.** **13 LLMs × 25 CSS benchmarks**, with a two-fold task-performance typology (taxonomic labeling vs. free-form generation). Closest template on *topic* (AI in social-science research workflows) and on explicit hypothesis-style claim framing ("what LLMs can and can't do yet"). Published in an ACL/MIT Press venue so the voice is more crisp than ORM would want, but the argument shape is informative.

**Features to borrow:**
- **"Augment the pipeline in two ways" style of conclusion** — crisp, numbered, bounded. The P1 conclusion currently reads more loosely; borrowing this structure would sharpen it.
- **Evaluation-pipeline framing** for the repository itself — describe the coding protocol as a reusable pipeline, not just as a one-off dataset.

**Risks / what *not* to borrow.** ACL voice; too benchmark-flavoured for ORM reviewers. Take the structure, not the tone.

### 6. Tay, Woo, Hickman, Booth & D'Mello (2022), *Advances in Methods and Practices in Psychological Science*

**Citation:** Tay, L., Woo, S. E., Hickman, L., Booth, B. M., & D'Mello, S. (2022). A conceptual framework for investigating and mitigating machine-learning measurement bias (MLMB) in psychological assessment. *Advances in Methods and Practices in Psychological Science*, 5(1). DOI: [10.1177/25152459211061337](https://doi.org/10.1177/25152459211061337). **Local PDF:** [`template_pdfs/tay_woo_hickman_booth_dmello_2022_AMPPS_MLMB_framework.pdf`](template_pdfs/tay_woo_hickman_booth_dmello_2022_AMPPS_MLMB_framework.pdf).

**Why.** Conceptual framework paper in a methods-adjacent venue. Defines a multi-level categorical scheme (types of measurement bias), links it to data-generating and modelling stages, and offers diagnostic criteria. Useful for the "define a categorical scheme + diagnostic criteria" move without requiring a case audit.

**Features to borrow:**
- **Diagnostic-criteria presentation of the typology** — each status is defined via observable behaviour (what would count as evidence this category applies).
- **Pairing with a canonical workflow diagram** — AMPPS favours figures; ORM allows them. One-figure taxonomy + workflow pairing would make the P1 typology land faster.

**Risks.** More conceptual and less empirical than ORM usually expects; don't let the framework become untethered from the 36 cases.

## Recommendation: which to mirror most closely

**Mirror Aguinis, Ramani & Alabduljader (2023) for overall structure.** It is the closest single template — same journal, same genre, same three-part contribution move, same "categorise + content-analyse + recommend" logic, comparable scale of evidence (168 reviews ≈ 36 cases × 17 roles = 612 observations). Three specific transplants that would be high-leverage in the next revision:

1. Rewrite the abstract's first half in their tripartite "First… Second… Third…" form.
2. Add a short **Exemplars** subsection (either in §3 before the prose paragraphs or as a supplemental table) naming one exemplar case per role status.
3. Reframe the conclusion to identify explicit stakeholder audiences (researchers / reviewers / institutions) and name the repository + codebook as the delivered artifact paired with the framework.

**Borrow the BARS move from Aguinis & Solarino (2019).** One half-page table giving the behavioural anchor for each of the six observable statuses (robust / conditional / transformed / shrinking / split_pressure / merge_pressure) materially addresses the single-coder concern and makes the typology replicable without opening the repository.

**Borrow the "two complementary lenses" framing from Hickman et al. (2022).** Present Tables 2a and 2b explicitly as complementary lenses on the same evidence, converging on the H3 verdict, rather than as two independent tables.

Tier-2 papers are useful for specific moves but not as overall structural templates.

## What I did not find

- **No ORM paper directly on AI/LLMs in the research workflow as a role-system audit** — the P1 manuscript is filling a gap, not imitating a predecessor. This is framing-positive if made explicit in §1.
- **No ORM paper that reports Cramér's V on a methodological typology** — your H3 test is novel in the venue. Include a short methods note ("We report χ² and Cramér's V on the status × case-family and status × capability contingency tables; Cramér's V is interpreted per Cohen 1988 as weak < 0.3, moderate 0.3–0.7, strong > 0.7") so reviewers don't bounce on unfamiliar usage.
- **No direct template for the sensitivity-analysis section.** Most methodological reviews don't do sensitivity analyses. That makes it an asset, not a gap; it's rigour above baseline.
