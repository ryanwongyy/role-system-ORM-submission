## Journal-style section skeleton

### 1. Introduction

Open with the methodological and practical stakes: social science research is increasingly entangled with AI systems, yet current debates mostly track capabilities, benchmark performance, or tool adoption rather than changes in the role system of research work. Then state the gap explicitly: the literature still lacks a comparative instrument for diagnosing whether automation claims imply persistence, transformation, redistribution, augmentation, or displacement of research roles across real cases.

- **Validated local PDFs:** `A1`, `A2`, `A5`, `A6`, `A7`, `L1`, `L3`, `L4`, `L5`
- **Manifest-only / should fetch:** `L2`
- **Preview-only / use cautiously:** none
- **Rhetorical job:** Move from “AI is advancing” to “the missing problem is role-system diagnosis.”

### 2. Conceptual Frame

Define the paper’s key terms tightly: research automation, research role, role status, role-set, and role-system change. Position the article at the intersection of AI-in-research, professions/expertise, role theory, and methodological-audit traditions so the reader sees the paper as diagnosing organized work rather than merely cataloguing tools.

- **Validated local PDFs:** `D1`, `D2`, `D6`, `E1`, `E4`, `C6`, `A1`
- **Manifest-only / should fetch:** `E2`, `E3`, `E5`
- **Preview-only / use cautiously:** `D4`, `D5`
- **Rhetorical job:** Give the reader a vocabulary for analyzing changing work arrangements before any counts appear.

### 3. Study Design and Case Corpus

Explain the 36-case corpus, the inclusion logic, and the unit of analysis in a way that reads as a disciplined comparative audit rather than a convenience sample. Clarify why case-based analysis is the right design for tracing role-system claims that are heterogeneous, public-facing, and often under-specified in their own materials.

- **Validated local PDFs:** `B1`, `B7`, `B4`, `J2`, `J3`, `J4`, `J5`, `K1`, `K2`, `K3`, `K5`
- **Manifest-only / should fetch:** `J1`, `K4`
- **Preview-only / use cautiously:** none
- **Rhetorical job:** Legitimate the corpus as a methodological design choice, not just an assembled archive.

### 4. Role-Status Framework

Present the role-status typology as the paper’s core analytic instrument and define each category in behaviorally legible terms. Make clear that the framework is designed to adjudicate claims about organized research work, not merely whether a tool can perform an isolated task in the abstract.

- **Validated local PDFs:** `C2`, `C3`, `C5`, `B6`, `F1`, `F2`, `F3`, `F4`, `F5`, `F6`, `G3`, `G4`, `G5`
- **Manifest-only / should fetch:** `C1`, `G1`, `G2`
- **Preview-only / use cautiously:** none
- **Rhetorical job:** Turn the typology into a usable methodological contribution rather than a descriptive coding convenience.

### 5. Evidence From the 36 Cases: Overall Pattern

Lead with the top-line pattern before showing internal detail: tell the reader which role statuses dominate across the corpus and what this implies about the shape of research automation. The goal of this section is to state the paper’s central empirical answer plainly before the analysis branches into subpatterns.

- **Validated local PDFs:** `A2`, `A5`, `A6`, `M1`, `M2`, `N1`, `N2`, `N3`, `N4`, `N5`
- **Manifest-only / should fetch:** none
- **Preview-only / use cautiously:** none
- **Rhetorical job:** Establish the empirical headline early so later sections read as elaboration rather than discovery.

Reorganize the same evidence by two analytic cuts, such as research stage and capability family, to show that the overall verdict is not an artifact of a single tabulation. Emphasize convergence between lenses: the reader should come away with the sense that multiple views of the corpus point to the same interpretation about augmentation, redistribution, and selective substitution.

Use a small number of sharply chosen cases to illustrate where automation pressures split roles, merge functions, or reassign jurisdiction rather than simply replacing tasks. The purpose here is not anecdotal color, but analytic concreteness: show how role-system change actually looks in practice when the general pattern becomes locally specific.

Translate the empirical findings into a diagnostic logic that readers can use when assessing future claims about automated research systems. Separate what the framework can adjudicate well — role persistence, redistribution, transformation, split pressure, merge pressure, augmentation claims — from what it cannot settle on its own, such as long-run institutional equilibrium or downstream social consequences.

Close with forward-looking implications for research practice, editorial evaluation, and institutional oversight, making clear that the paper opens a line of inquiry about role-system change rather than closing it.
